The Pitch Framework

5 parts. ~5 minutes. Maximum impact.

Every winning YIC pitch follows this structure. Click each part to see how to nail it.

🎯

Part 1 — The Hook (first 30 seconds)

Open with something real. A story. A statistic. A question that makes the judge stop and listen.

Presentation 10% Problem Statement 15%
⏱ Aim for 30 – 45 seconds
✓ Strong hook

"Last year, 3 farmers in our village lost their entire harvest because sun bears entered their fields at night — and no one knew until morning. We decided to change that."

✗ Weak hook

"Hello judges. Our project is about sun bears. We chose this because we thought it was interesting and also related to the environment."

Pro tip: Start with a real person, a real moment, or a real number — not a dictionary definition. Judges hear hundreds of "our project is about..." openings.
  • Can you name one specific person or community who suffers from this problem?
  • What's the most dramatic or surprising fact you found in your research?
  • Can you paint a scene in under two sentences that makes the judge feel the problem?
🔍

Part 2 — The Problem (deep dive)

Show the judge you truly understand who is suffering, why it matters, and that you did real community research — not just Googled it.

Problem Statement 15% Effectiveness 10%
⏱ Aim for 45 – 60 seconds
✓ Strong problem

"We surveyed 12 farmers in Sabah. 9 of them said they had lost income due to sun bear raids. The problem: no affordable early-warning system exists. Existing solutions cost over RM5,000 — far beyond what rural farmers can afford."

✗ Weak problem

"Sun bears are endangered. This is bad for the environment. People need to care more about nature. We want to help protect them."

The 3-part problem formula: Who is affected? → What specifically goes wrong? → Why does nothing currently fix it?
  • Did you talk to or survey real people from this community? Mention it.
  • Can you quantify the problem — numbers, frequency, cost, people affected?
  • What's the specific gap that your solution fills that others don't?
📊

Part 3 — Your Data Story

This is worth 15% alone. Show how you collected real data, what it means, and how it trained your AI model. This proves your science is legitimate.

Data Strategy 15% Coding 5% Innovation 10%
⏱ Aim for 60 – 90 seconds
✓ Strong data story

"We collected 320 images of sun bear footprints using camera traps over 3 weeks. We cleaned out blurry images, labelled each one, then trained a TFLite model using Teachable Machine. Our test accuracy was 87%."

✗ Weak data story

"We used Teachable Machine. We uploaded pictures from the internet and trained it. The AI can now recognise sun bears."

Cover all 4 data Ds: Discover (where you got data) → Describe (what it contains) → Deduce (what you cleaned/processed) → Deploy (how it trained the model)
  • Did you collect your own data, or responsibly source it? Explain which and why.
  • How many data samples? What was your train/test split?
  • Did you face any data challenges (imbalance, noise, limited samples)? What did you do?
🤖

Part 4 — Your AI Solution

Show the prototype working. Explain how the Edge AI runs on your hardware. Convince judges this actually solves the problem.

Functionality 10% Feasibility 5% Innovation 10% Effectiveness 10%
⏱ Aim for 75 – 90 seconds
✓ Strong tech pitch

"Our ESP32-based device runs our TFLite model offline — no internet needed. When it detects a sun bear signature in heat sensor data, it sends an alert via LoRa to the farmer's basic phone within 2 seconds."

✗ Weak tech pitch

"We made an app that uses AI. When the AI detects something, it sends a notification. It's made using Python and also connects to the internet."

Live demo beats everything. If your prototype works, show it running — even a short 30-second live demo scores higher than any slide.
  • What hardware/software does your Edge AI run on? Say it by name (ESP32, Raspberry Pi, Android, etc.).
  • What happens if there's no internet? Does your solution still work?
  • What makes your approach different from something that already exists?
✨

Part 5 — Reflect, Impact & Future

This section is 30% of your score. Don't rush it. Judges want to see how you think, what you learned, and where this is going.

Learning 10% Teamwork 5% Future Thinking 5% Effectiveness 10%
⏱ Aim for 60 – 75 seconds
✓ Strong reflection

"We failed our first model — only 52% accuracy. That taught us to diversify our data angles. Aina handled ML training, Haziq did hardware, I handled community interviews. Our biggest learning: talk to users early, not just at the end."

✗ Weak reflection

"We worked well together as a team. We learned a lot about AI. In future, we would improve our project by making it better and adding more features."

Real > perfect. Sharing a genuine failure and what you learned from it scores higher than pretending everything went smoothly. Judges respect honesty.
  • What was one specific thing that didn't work, and what did you do about it?
  • What is each team member's name and role? Mention all three.
  • If you had 6 more months, what would you add or change?
  • Who in the real world will use this, and how will their life improve?

Delivery Tips

On the day.

The content wins points. The delivery wins hearts.

👁

Eye contact beats slides

Talk to the judges, not your slides. If you're reading off the screen, you've lost them. Know your content well enough to glance, not stare.

🎭

Split roles for impact

Assign speaking sections to different team members. One person for the problem, one for the tech demo, one for reflection. Judges love seeing all three voices.

⏱

Rehearse with a timer

Time your pitch until it fits. Going overtime signals poor preparation. Under-running signals shallow content. Hit 4–5 minutes consistently.

❓

Prepare for Q&A

Judges will ask hard questions. Practise "I don't know, but here's how I'd find out" — it's more impressive than making up an answer.

📱

Demo early, demo live

Show your prototype working as early as possible. Even a 20-second live demo changes the energy in the room completely.

💚

Own your passion

Judges can tell when a team genuinely cares about their cause. Speak about your community partner, your research interviews, your real users — that energy is contagious and memorable.
